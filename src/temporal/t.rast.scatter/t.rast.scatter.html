DESCRIPTION

t.rast.scatter draws a scatterplot of the values of two space-time raster datasets (strds) against one another. Both datasets are sampled at the same set of points (supplied as a vector point map) at every shared timestep, so each plotted dot is a matched (x, y) pair: the value of the first dataset and the value of the second dataset at one location and one moment in time. Optionally, one or more regression curves are fitted to the cloud of points and drawn on top: an ordinary least-squares (OLS) line, a LOWESS smoother, and/or a Generalized Additive Model (GAM) curve.

The module is a standalone tool for exploratory analysis of the relationship between two co-located variables through time, for example a vegetation index against land surface temperature, or rainfall against soil moisture. Internally it uses t.rast.what to sample both datasets at the given points across all timesteps, pairs the two series in time, and then plots the result with matplotlib.

Unlike a time-series plot, which shows each variable along a time axis, a scatterplot shows the two variables against each other. This makes it suited to questions about co-variation: do high values of one go with high values of the other, is the relationship linear or curved, does it tighten or loosen, and do the points trace a loop over the seasons.

Input and sampling

The two datasets are given with xstrds (plotted on the x axis) and ystrds (plotted on the y axis). Both must use absolute time (see below).

The sample locations are supplied with points, a vector map of points. Both datasets are sampled at exactly these locations, and the same points are reused at every timestep, so the pairing of (x, y) values is always at an identical location. Only point features are used. The points are passed straight to t.rast.what, which samples at the current computational region's resolution; set the region with g.region before running if needed.

The points can be any vector map you like. To sample a region at random, create the points beforehand with v.random (optionally restricted to a vector area, or to the non-null cells of a raster), and pass the result as points. Keeping point generation outside the module means you stay in full control of how and where the sampling happens.

Absolute time only

The tool supports absolute-time (calendar-dated) datasets only. Relative-time datasets are intentionally not supported: without a known time unit a tolerance such as 1 day is meaningless, and there is no reliable way to tell whether two relative indices refer to genuinely comparable moments. Rather than risk silently pairing unrelated observations, the module stops with an error if either dataset uses relative time.

Pairing the two datasets in time

The two datasets rarely have identical timestamps, so the method option controls how their timesteps are matched into (x, y) pairs. There are two distinct strategies.

method=nearest (the default) matches each map of one dataset to the closest-in-time map of the other, within a maximum allowed time difference set by tolerance. This preserves the individual observations: every paired dot is a real sample from each dataset. The tolerance is a duration string such as 1 day, 6 hours or 30 minutes; a map with no counterpart inside that window is dropped from the plot. Matching is nearest-neighbour and one-to-one, so an observation is never reused. Use this when each map is a discrete acquisition (for example a satellite overpass) and the timestamp differences are small jitter rather than a difference in sampling design.

The aggregate methods (mean, median, sum, min, max) take a different approach: both datasets are first binned to a common frequency and each bin is reduced to a single summary value, and then the per-period summaries are paired. Here a plotted dot is no longer a single matched observation but a pair of period summaries (for example the monthly mean of each dataset). The frequency is a pandas offset alias such as D (daily), W (weekly), MS (month start) or YS (year start). Use this when the two datasets have different native cadences, or when you are interested in the relationship between typical per-period values rather than between individual acquisitions.

The two strategies are mutually exclusive in their settings: tolerance applies only to method=nearest, and frequency only to the aggregate methods. Supplying the wrong companion option, or omitting the required one, is reported as an error.

In both cases the pairing keeps only point/time combinations where both datasets have a non-null value. Maps or bins present in only one dataset contribute nothing to the scatter and are dropped.

Regression curves

Three optional fits can be drawn over the scatter cloud, each enabled by a flag.

-o adds an ordinary least-squares (OLS) regression line, reported with its slope, intercept and R² (SciPy's linregress).

-l adds a LOWESS (locally weighted scatterplot smoothing) curve, a flexible local regression that follows the data without assuming a global functional shape (statsmodels' lowess). Its smoothness is set with lowess_frac, the fraction of the data used to estimate each local value (between 0 and 1, default 0.3); larger values give a smoother curve.

-g adds a Generalized Additive Model (GAM) curve, a smooth spline fit (pyGAM). It captures curved relationships without prescribing their form.

The -t flag adds the fit statistics (such as the OLS slope and R²) to the plot legend. The statistics are always printed to the terminal regardless of this flag.

A note on significance. A scatterplot of two time series invites caution. Environmental series are almost always serially autocorrelated, which means successive points are not independent. The OLS slope and its R² therefore describe the visible association but should not be read as a formal hypothesis test: the effective sample size is smaller than the number of dots, and ordinary significance assessments will be over-optimistic. Treat the fits as descriptive summaries of co-variation, not as proof of a relationship.

Colouring the points

By default all points are drawn in a single colour, set with color. It accepts a standard GRASS colour name (such as blue), an R:G:B triplet with each component in 0-255 (such as 51:125:255), a hex code (#1f77b4) or a tab: name.

The -c flag instead colours each point by its timestep, with a colorbar mapping colour to date. This reveals temporal structure that a single-colour scatter hides, for example a seasonal hysteresis loop where the relationship follows a different path on the way up than on the way down. When -c is set, the color option is ignored.

Output

By default the scatterplot is shown in an interactive window. If output is given, the plot is instead saved to that file and the image format is taken from the file extension (.png, .pdf, .svg, ...). Plot appearance is controlled by dpi (resolution, default 300) and plot_dimensions (width,height in inches).

The backend option selects the matplotlib rendering backend. You rarely need it: Agg is chosen automatically when writing to a file, and WXAgg (an interactive window) when no output file is given. Override it only if your system needs a different interactive backend (e.g. TkAgg, Qt5Agg).

The csv option additionally writes the paired (x, y) samples, one row per point and timestep with their coordinates and date, so they can be replotted or analysed in the software tool of your choice.

Subsetting and performance

The where option accepts a temporal WHERE clause (as used by other t.* modules) to restrict the analysis to a subset of timestamps, for example a particular range of years.

The nprocs option sets the number of parallel processes used during sampling by t.rast.what, potentially making the module substantially faster to run.

NOTES

This addon requires the Python packages numpy, pandas, scipy and matplotlib. They are all pip-installable, e.g. 
pip install numpy pandas scipy matplotlib

A recent pandas (version 2.0 or newer) is required. The optional regression curves bring two further, also optional, dependencies: statsmodels for the LOWESS smoother (-l) and pygam for the GAM curve (-g). 

pip install statsmodels pygam

All dependencies are checked up front, before any sampling is done, so a missing package fails immediately rather than after the (potentially slow) sampling step. The optional packages are only required when their flag is set: you can run the module without statsmodels or pygam as long as you do not request the corresponding curve.

Computation depends on the following libraries:

  • pandas builds the time-indexed series, performs the nearest-in-time pairing (pandas.merge_asof) or the aggregation-then-pairing, and assembles the paired table.
  • scipy fits the OLS regression line (scipy.stats.linregress).
  • statsmodels fits the optional LOWESS smoother (lowess).
  • pygam fits the optional GAM curve (LinearGAM).
  • numpy provides the array operations underlying the above, and matplotlib renders the scatterplot.

EXAMPLES

The examples use the North Carolina mapset with climatic data time series (nc_climate_spm_2000_2012), which you can download from the GRASS sample data page.

Data preparation

The following is borrowed from the NSCU-Geoforall tutorial. We create temporal datasets which serve as containers for the time series. First step is to create empty datasets of type strds (space-time raster dataset). Note, that we use absolute time. 
t.create output=tempmean type=strds temporaltype=absolute title="Average
temperature" description="Monthly temperature average in NC [deg C]"

t.create output=precip_sum type=strds temporaltype=absolute
title="Precipitation" description="Monthly precipitation sum in NC [mm]"

Now we register raster maps into the space-time raster datasets we just created. We use g.list to list separately temperature and precipitation maps. Note that g.list lists maps alphabetically which in this case orders the maps chronologically which is what we need. Using backticks to pass the maps directly to t.register 

t.register -i input=tempmean type=raster start=2000-01-01 increment="1 months"
maps=`g.list type=raster pattern="*tempmean" separator=comma --quiet`

t.register -i input=precip_sum type=raster start=2000-01-01 increment="1 months"
maps=`g.list type=raster pattern="*precip" separator=comma --quiet`

Create sample points

The module samples both datasets at a set of points. Here we generate 100 random points across the current region with v.random, to be reused at every timestep: 
g.region raster=2000_01_tempmean -p

v.random output=samples npoints=100 seed=1

Scatterplot of two datasets

Plot the monthly temperature against the monthly precipitation at the sample points. Because the two datasets share the same monthly timestamps, a small tolerance is enough to pair them: 
t.rast.scatter xstrds=tempmean ystrds=precip_sum points=samples \ tolerance="5
days" output=t_rast_scatter_01.png

Resulting image:

!Scatterplot of monthly temperature against precipitation

Add regression curves

To explore the form of the relationship, add an OLS line (-o) and a LOWESS smoother (-l), and show the fit statistics in the legend with -t: 
t.rast.scatter -olt xstrds=tempmean ystrds=precip_sum points=samples \
tolerance="5 days" output=t_rast_scatter_02.png

Resulting image:

!Scatterplot with OLS line and LOWESS smoother

Colour the points by time

A single-colour scatter hides any seasonal structure. Add -c to colour each point by its date, which can reveal a seasonal loop in the relationship: 
t.rast.scatter -c xstrds=tempmean ystrds=precip_sum points=samples \
tolerance="5 days" output=t_rast_scatter_03.png

Resulting image:

!Scatterplot with points coloured by time

Aggregate before pairing

When you are interested in the relationship between typical per-period values rather than between individual acquisitions, switch from nearest-in-time matching to aggregation. Here both datasets are binned to year start (YS) and reduced to their annual mean before pairing: 
t.rast.scatter -ot xstrds=tempmean ystrds=precip_sum points=samples \
  method=mean frequency=YS output=t_rast_scatter_04.png

Each dot now represents one point in one year, with the x and y values being that year's mean temperature and mean precipitation at that location.

Restrict to a subset of years

The where option restricts the analysis to a subset of timestamps, for example the years 2005 to 2010: 
t.rast.scatter -ol xstrds=tempmean ystrds=precip_sum points=samples \
tolerance="5 days" where="start_time >= '2005-01-01' and start_time <
'2011-01-01'" \ output=t_rast_scatter_05.png
